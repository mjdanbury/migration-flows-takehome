Getting Out Of Town

Where Americans have ditched urban hubs for suburban comfort, immigrants see a chance at a new life.

By Matthew Danbury

Americans are continuing their yearslong flight from major urban centers, according to new 2024 population estimates from the U.S. Census Bureau.

Unlike previous years, these urban departures have been largely offset by a surge in migration from other countries.

Yet with the Trump administration’s aggressive policies to curtail immigration, it is uncertain whether these gains will be a long term fix for big cities whose post-covid populations are finding home elsewhere.

Cities across the US — including New York, Chicago, Los Angeles, Miami, Dallas, Atlanta, Boston, San Francisco, Philadelphia, and Washington D.C. — saw domestic migration away from their counties.

Their losses were often neighboring counties’ gains.

Many of these losses come on the heels of losses of similar size as the COVID-19 pandemic and a shift to remote and hybrid work has reshaped people’s decision framework for where to call home.

For the first time in recent years, these losses were largely offset by an influx of international migration.

But this relief to cities in the form of a larger base of people to work, pay taxes, and spend money may be short lived. With the deportations, travel restrictions, and restrictions on student visas imposed by the Trump administration, it is unclear whether this offset will persist into 2025.
